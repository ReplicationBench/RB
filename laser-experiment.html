ABOUT THIS EXPERIMENT

What this experiment measures

A laser fires horizontally east from a fixed mount on a ridge. A drone equipped with a quadrant beam sensor flies downrange, correcting its position to keep the beam centered on its sensor. The drone has no compass, no map, no programmed direction. It only knows whether the beam is centered. Wherever the beam goes, the drone goes.

What this experiment proves and doesn't prove

This is not a single decisive test of Earth's shape. It is a controlled measurement of three independent variables — Polaris angle, drone altitude above ground, and laser tilt angle — across a measured baseline distance. The pattern across all three readings tells you what the surface did underneath the beam path.

A single run does not settle the question. Repeat runs in different atmospheric conditions, at different latitudes, and in different seasons strengthen the evidence considerably. The protocol is reproducible by anyone with a few hundred dollars in equipment and access to open terrain.

WHY USE A DRONE INSTEAD OF A PERSON

A human walker introduces dozens of micro-decisions per second — pace, gait, where to step around obstacles, how to interpret a dim signal. The drone has one instruction: keep the beam centered on the sensor. The flight controller corrects mechanically without interpretation, removing observer bias from the experiment entirely.

532 NM GREEN LASER

Green wavelengths are the brightest visible light per watt of power and remain visible in clear air at long range. Silicon photodiodes — the kind used in the drone's quadrant sensor — are sensitive to 532 nm. A 200mW unit is the practical floor for daytime visibility at distance; 500mW is better for ranges beyond 5 miles. Higher powers exist but require eye-safety protocols and may be regulated.

MOTORISED PAN/TILT MOUNT

The mount must lock the horizontal (yaw) axis absolutely while allowing free vertical (pitch) movement. A tilt encoder logs the pitch angle continuously — this is the source of Measurement 3. Quality matters: cheap mounts have backlash that introduces angular noise comparable to the signal you are looking for. Look for backlash under 0.05 degrees.

QUADRANT BEAM SENSOR

Four photodiodes arranged in a cross around a central aperture. When the beam strikes the center, all four read equal intensity. When the beam drifts up, the top two read brighter. The differential signal tells the flight controller which way to correct. This is the elegant part of the design — the drone never needs to know which direction is east. It only needs to keep all four photodiodes balanced.

OPTICAL FLOW + DOWNWARD RANGEFINDER

Combined modules like the Matek 3901-L0X include both a downward-pointing camera (for GPS-independent position tracking via ground texture) and a laser rangefinder (for altitude above ground). One module gives you distance traveled along the path AND the continuous altitude reading needed for Measurement 2. Crucially, this is fully independent of GPS or any satellite system; it measures the ground directly.

CLINOMETER

A digital inclinometer accurate to 0.1 degrees is sufficient to detect Polaris angle changes corresponding to roughly 7 miles of north/south displacement. Sub-arcminute precision is achievable with sextants but adds cost and skill requirements. The 0.1 degree resolution is the practical floor for a reproducible amateur protocol.

THE GEOMETRY

The diagram shows a side view of the experiment. The operator stands on a ridge — high enough that the laser clears any intermediate terrain and the horizon is not a factor. The drone hovers at the same elevation as the beam at the start.

If the surface beneath the beam is flat, the drone holds its starting altitude throughout the flight. The beam stays parallel to the ground. The tilt encoder reports zero. The clinometer reads the same angle to Polaris at the end as at the start.

If the surface beneath the beam curves away, the ground drops below where it began. The drone must climb to stay on the beam — because the beam is a straight line and the surface is not. The downward rangefinder logs the climb. The tilt encoder logs the operator tilting upward to track the climbing drone. The clinometer reads a different angle to Polaris because the drone has displaced along the surface.

WHY THE RIDGE MATTERS

Beam-blocking by intervening terrain is a real problem at long range. A ridge of even modest height — 50 to 200 metres — clears most natural obstructions and puts the beam well above the boundary layer where thermal refraction is strongest. The ridge also gives the operator and equipment a stable platform that won't shift during the run.

WHY EAST

East was chosen because it keeps the beam roughly tangent to a latitude line if the surface is curved. North or south would introduce latitude change directly into the beam path, mixing variables. East-west keeps the curvature signal geometrically clean. The choice of east specifically (rather than west) is arbitrary — west would work identically.

STEP 01 — RECORDING ALPHA

Both operator and drone are at the same starting position on the ridge. A single Polaris reading is taken with both parties present. This eliminates instrument disagreement at the baseline — both teams agree on the starting value because they witnessed it together. Polaris is chosen because it is essentially fixed above the celestial north pole; its angle above the horizon corresponds to the observer's geographic latitude.

STEP 02 — FIRING THE LASER EAST

The laser mount's horizontal axis is locked. This is mechanical, not procedural — physical clamps prevent any yaw movement during the run. The vertical axis remains free because the drone may move up or down as it follows the beam, and the operator must track it. The tilt encoder logs every vertical adjustment.

The first beam alignment uses a compass to point east. This is the only role the compass plays. Once the beam is established and locked, the compass is removed. From that moment forward the beam itself defines the path — not the compass, not any map, not any model.

STEP 03 — FOLLOWING THE BEAM

The drone's flight controller is wired only to the quadrant sensor output. There is no waypoint navigation, no GPS guidance, no programmed heading. The single instruction is to hold the beam centered on the sensor face. If the beam drifts up on the sensor, the drone climbs. If it drifts left, the drone moves left. Altitude and lateral position are determined entirely by where the beam points.

The optical flow module logs distance traveled along the actual path — not a straight-line distance assumption. If the beam curves the drone's path with it, the odometer reads the path length.

Stopping distance should be agreed before the run begins. Distance is bounded by the laser's effective range on the night's atmospheric conditions. On a clear low-humidity night a 200mW laser remains coherent to roughly 20-30 miles. On hazy or humid nights this drops to 5-10 miles.

STEP 04 — THREE READINGS AT END POSITION

When the drone reaches the agreed distance it lands. The chase crew on site takes a fresh Polaris reading using the same clinometer and the same method as in Step 01. The drone's altitude log is downloaded. The laser tilt encoder reading is recorded from the operator's mount.

These three values — BETA, final altitude, and final tilt — combined with the corresponding starting values, are the entire output of the experiment.

WHY THREE MEASUREMENTS AND NOT ONE

Any single instrument can be wrong. A clinometer can have a calibration error, an altitude rangefinder can have a software bias, an encoder can slip. By logging three independent quantities that should agree if the underlying signal is real, the experiment becomes self-checking.

Three readings from three different physical principles — angular sighting of a star, distance measurement to the ground, mechanical encoding of laser pitch — should all tell the same story. If any one disagrees with the other two, that instrument or that step in the procedure needs investigation before the run is trusted.

MEASUREMENT 1 — POLARIS ANGLE

Polaris sits within about half a degree of the celestial north pole. Its angle above the horizon at any location is essentially equal to that location's geographic latitude. A change in Polaris angle between start and end positions corresponds directly to a change in latitude — a north/south displacement along the surface.

This measurement has a known limitation: some models attribute different geometric meaning to Polaris observations. The strength of the experiment is not in this single measurement but in its consistency with Measurements 2 and 3.

MEASUREMENT 2 — DRONE ALTITUDE ABOVE GROUND

This is the most geometrically unambiguous of the three. The drone is following a perfectly straight beam of light. If the ground beneath it stays at the same distance below the beam throughout the flight, the surface is flat over that distance. If the ground drops away from the beam, the surface curves.

No interpretation of star positions is required. No assumptions about light propagation through space. Just a downward-pointing rangefinder logging distance to the ground beneath the drone, second by second, for the entire flight.

MEASUREMENT 3 — LASER TILT ANGLE

This is geometrically equivalent to Measurement 2 but logged from the other end of the beam. If the drone climbs, the operator must tilt the laser upward to track it. The tilt encoder records that adjustment. The total tilt change from start to end is a direct measure of the angle subtended by the drone's altitude gain at the operator's position.

Because it measures the same underlying phenomenon as Measurement 2 from a different vantage point, it serves as a cross-check. If Measurement 2 says the drone climbed 600 feet and Measurement 3 says it climbed 100 feet, something is wrong with one of the instruments.

INTERPRETING THE RESULTS

Outcome A across all three measurements indicates no detectable curvature signal over the run distance. Outcome B across all three indicates curvature consistent with a spherical surface. Outcome C indicates either northward displacement (Polaris) or a downward-bowing surface (altitude) — both of which would be unexpected and warrant investigation rather than conclusion.

Disagreement between the three measurements is a signal that the experiment was compromised somehow. Disagreement should always trigger a repeat run with attention to whatever instrument or condition might have caused the discrepancy.

HANDLING ATMOSPHERIC REFRACTION

Atmospheric refraction is the strongest objection to this experiment and deserves direct treatment. Light bends as it passes through air of varying density, and that bending can mimic or mask the signal the experiment is trying to measure.

RUN IN MULTIPLE CONDITIONS

Refraction varies with temperature gradient, humidity, and pressure. If the altitude gain on a cold dry winter night matches the altitude gain on a warm humid summer night within a small margin, refraction is not the dominant cause. If the readings vary wildly with weather, refraction is doing real work and the signal is unreliable.

MEASURE REFRACTION DIRECTLY

A second laser fired between two fixed points of known elevation and distance during the same atmospheric conditions gives a direct refraction measurement. Standard surveying uses reciprocal leveling — two observers measure each other simultaneously from known points, and any discrepancy beyond the known geometry is refraction. Log the coefficient during the experiment and subtract it from the altitude reading.

USE MULTIPLE WAVELENGTHS

Refraction is wavelength-dependent. Red light bends less than blue light through the same atmosphere. Running two beams simultaneously — a red laser and a green laser tracking the same drone — produces measurable differences in their tilt angles or the drone's relative position if refraction is occurring. If both beams agree perfectly, refraction is negligible. If they diverge, you have measured the refraction directly.

CHOOSE THE NIGHT CAREFULLY

Refraction is strongest in the boundary layer near the ground where temperature gradients are steepest. A cold clear night with low humidity minimizes the variable. Beam paths well above ground level — facilitated by the ridge mount — keep the experiment out of the worst refractive zone. Avoid running during sunrise or sunset transitions when thermal gradients are maximum.

REVERSE THE EXPERIMENT

Have the laser at the far point fire back to the origin, with a second drone repeating the run in the opposite direction. If both runs show the same altitude gain pattern, refraction is symmetric and largely cancels. If the two runs disagree, you have either an instrument problem or asymmetric atmospheric conditions worth investigating before drawing conclusions.

THE HONEST LIMIT

You cannot eliminate refraction without going to vacuum. But you can bound it, measure it, and correct for it well enough that the residual is small compared to the curvature signal you are looking for. A run with multi-wavelength measurement, conducted from a high ridge on a cold clear night, with reciprocal verification, drives the refraction contribution well below the curvature contribution at moderate distances.

Refraction is also not the friend it is sometimes claimed to be. Standard atmospheric refraction bends light downward, which means objects appear higher than they geometrically should — partially compensating for curvature, not adding to it. Any claim that refraction explains away a curvature observation requires the world to look more curved than it actually is, which is the opposite of what refraction does.
