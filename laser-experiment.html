ABOUT THIS EXPERIMENT

What this measures

A laser points east and holds still. A drone hangs in the air in front of it, reading nothing but the beam on its sensor. No map. No compass. No heading typed into a flight computer. When the beam drifts up the sensor face, the drone climbs to meet it. When it drifts sideways, the drone slides over. Wherever the light goes, the machine goes.

The experiment does not assume an answer. It records what the surface does beneath a straight line of light, and it reports whatever the numbers report. Flat is a result. Curved is a result. Neither is the goal.

What it can show, and what it cannot

This is not the one test that ends an argument. There is no such test. What it is, is a measurement of three quantities that are independent of one another: the angle to Polaris, the drone's height above the ground, and the tilt of the laser at its mount. Each can come out level, or changing, or somewhere that needs explaining. Read together, across a measured distance, they describe what the surface did. Nothing more, and nothing less.

One run settles nothing on its own. Run it again. On another night, in another season, from another latitude, and the reading either holds or it does not. That is the only test a result has to pass: whether it survives being repeated. The cost of admission is a few hundred dollars and a stretch of open ground, which is the point. Anyone can check the work.

WHY NO MAP

A map is not the world. It is a picture of the world, and every map is built from choices: what to keep, what to leave out, which projection to flatten it through, which names to print. That holds for a hand sketch, and it holds for a satellite mosaic, which is stitched, reprojected, and color-corrected before anyone sees it. No map escapes those choices, and it is fair to distrust any claim that leans on one (Harley, 1989). This protocol leans on none. The drone is told nothing about where the world goes. It follows a beam of light and writes down physical quantities: an angle to a star, a height above the ground, the tilt of a mount, the temperature and pressure of the air. A measurement carries assumptions of its own, and the answer to that is not to pretend it does not. It is to name them, and to read instruments that fail in different ways, so no single assumption decides the result. What the protocol asks you to trust is not a chart, and not a picture of the Earth taken by anyone. It is the reading built in front of you.

WHY A DRONE, NOT A PERSON

A person walking makes a thousand small choices a second. Where to step. How fast. Whether that faint smear of green is the beam or just a tired eye. Every choice is a place where expectation can steer the outcome. The drone makes one choice and makes it again: keep the beam centered. The flight controller does it mechanically, with no stake in the answer. It cannot hope for one. That is the reason it holds the instrument and not a person.

532 NM GREEN LASER

Green is the brightest visible color per watt, and it carries through clear air at range. The silicon photodiodes in the drone's sensor respond well at 532 nm, so the beam the eye sees and the beam the sensor reads are the same beam. A 200 mW unit is the practical floor for daytime work; 500 mW buys margin past 5 miles. More power than that means eye-safety protocols, and in some places, permits.

MOTORISED PAN/TILT MOUNT

The mount has one demand on it. Lock the horizontal axis dead still, and leave the vertical axis free to move. A tilt encoder logs the vertical angle the whole time. That log is Measurement 3. Quality earns its place here: a cheap mount has backlash, and backlash is angular noise sitting right on top of the signal you came to read. Look for backlash under 0.05 degrees.

QUADRANT BEAM SENSOR

Four photodiodes in a cross, around a center aperture. Beam on center, all four read the same. Beam drifts up, the top pair brightens. The difference between them tells the flight controller which way to nudge. This is the quiet trick of the whole rig: the drone never has to know which way is east. It only has to keep four numbers equal.

OPTICAL FLOW + DOWNWARD RANGEFINDER

A combined module like the Matek 3901-L0X carries two instruments looking down. A camera tracks position by reading the texture of the ground passing beneath it. A laser rangefinder reads the height to that ground. Together they give you distance traveled along the real path and the running altitude that becomes Measurement 2. Both look at the ground directly. Neither asks a satellite anything.

CLINOMETER

A digital inclinometer good to 0.1 degrees is enough. That resolution catches a Polaris shift of about 7 miles of north/south travel, which is the scale this protocol works at. Sextants reach finer, into the arcminutes, but they cost more and ask more of the hands holding them. For a protocol meant to be repeated by ordinary people, 0.1 degrees is the floor that still works.

SAFETY, MOUNTING, AND LOGGING

A 200 mW green laser is bright enough to injure an eye, so everyone on the crew wears 532 nm goggles rated OD 4 or better, and the beam is kept above head height once it is lit. The mount rides on a surveyor tripod with a leveling head, because a beam that drifts only because its platform sagged is noise you cannot take back out later. And the origin needs a small datalogger with a microSD card to timestamp the mount's weather and tilt readings, so the two ends of the run can be lined up second for second when it is over.

WEATHER SENSORS (BOTH ENDS)

A combined module like the BME280 reads temperature, relative humidity, and barometric pressure from one small chip, for a few dollars. Carry one at the origin, mounted at beam height on the ridge, and one on the drone at the far end. Those three numbers fix the refractivity of the air the beam crosses: N = 77.6·P/T + 3.73 × 10⁵ · e/T², where P is the pressure in hPa, T the temperature in kelvin, and e the water-vapor pressure that the humidity reading gives (Hirt et al., 2010). Logged at both ends through the run, they turn the refraction correction from an estimate into a measured quantity. Let the flight controller record the drone's and add a small datalogger at the mount; two modules and a logger cost about the price of a meal.

OPTIONAL EXTRAS FOR THE REFRACTION CONTROLS

The refraction section describes checks that ask for a little more gear. A second laser in another color, red at 635 nm, runs the two-color test for roughly $40 to $90. A second drone, about the price of the first, lets you fly the run in reverse at the same time. Neither is needed for a first run. Both turn a bounded estimate of the bending into a measured one, so budget for them if the early numbers come out close.

THE GEOMETRY

The diagram is a side view. The operator stands on a ridge, high enough that the beam clears whatever terrain lies between the two ends and the horizon never enters the picture. The drone starts at the elevation of the beam.

Hold two possibilities side by side, and give each the same weight. If the surface beneath the beam is flat, the drone keeps its starting altitude the whole way. The beam runs parallel to the ground. The tilt encoder stays at zero. The clinometer reads the same angle to Polaris at the end as it did at the start.

If the surface curves away instead, the ground falls below where it began. The drone climbs to stay on the beam, because the beam is a straight line and the ground is not. The rangefinder logs the climb. The encoder logs the operator tilting up to follow. The clinometer reads a different angle to Polaris, because the drone has moved along the surface. The protocol does not decide between these in advance. It builds the apparatus that can tell them apart, and then it reads the dials.

WHY THE RIDGE MATTERS

Over long distances a hill or a swell in the land can block the beam, and a blocked beam ends the run. A ridge of even modest height, 50 to 200 metres, clears most of that. It also lifts the beam above the boundary layer near the ground, where the temperature gradient and the bending it causes are strongest (Young, n.d.). And it gives the equipment a platform that will not shift underneath it during the run.

WHY EAST

East keeps the beam running roughly along a line of latitude. Point the beam north or south and a latitude change rides directly into the path, tangling the variables together. East and west keep that one signal clean. East over west is arbitrary. West would do the same job.

A side view, the drone flying east with the beam arriving from the west. Every instrument on it points at a single thing, and none of them points at a map.

QUADRANT BEAM SENSOR (REAR)

The sensor faces back along the beam, toward the origin in the west. Four photodiodes around a center aperture read the light; when all four match, the beam is centered, and keeping them matched is the flight controller's whole job. Mounting it at the rear keeps it square to the incoming beam through the run.

RETROREFLECTOR (REAR)

Beside the sensor, a retroreflector sends part of the beam straight back to the origin. That return is what the operator's encoder tracks for the tilt reading, Measurement 3, and it lets a reciprocal refraction check work from the ground end.

OPTICAL FLOW + RANGEFINDER (UNDERSIDE)

Both look straight down. The optical-flow camera reads the texture of the ground sliding past to count the real distance traveled, and the laser rangefinder reads the height to the ground, which is Measurement 2. Neither asks anything of a satellite.

WEATHER SENSOR (TOP MAST)

Temperature, humidity, and pressure ride on a short mast above the body, up out of the rotor wash where the air is still the air the beam crossed. Those readings fix the refractivity at the drone's end, and sampled at two heights, the vertical gradient that bends the light.

FLIGHT CONTROLLER AND BATTERY (CORE)

The flight controller sits in the center, wired to one input only: the beam sensor. No compass, no satellite receiver, no stored heading. The battery sits beside it. Everything the machine knows about where to go, it learns from the light on the sensor.

STEP 01 — RECORDING ALPHA

Operator and drone start at the same spot on the ridge. One Polaris reading is taken with both parties standing there to witness it. That removes any argument about the baseline later, because no one is taking the starting number on faith. Polaris sits within about half a degree of the celestial north pole, and its height above the horizon runs close to the observer's latitude (Bowditch, 2019). That is the value the experiment watches for change.

STEP 02 — FIRING THE LASER EAST

The horizontal axis of the mount is locked. Locked mechanically, with clamps, not locked by a promise to be careful. The vertical axis stays free, because the drone may rise or fall as it follows the beam and the operator has to follow it. The encoder writes down every vertical move.

A compass points the first shot east. That is the only thing the compass does. Once the beam is set and the axis is clamped, the compass comes off the mount. From there on, the beam is the path. Not the compass. Not a map. Not a model of what the path should be.

STEP 03 — FOLLOWING THE BEAM

The flight controller is wired to one input: the quadrant sensor. No waypoints. No satellite guidance. No heading to hold. The single instruction is to keep the beam in the center of the sensor. Beam rides up, the drone climbs. Beam slides left, the drone goes left. Where the drone ends up, in height and in line, is set entirely by where the light goes.

The optical-flow module logs the distance actually traveled, not a straight-line guess. If the path bends, the odometer counts the length of the bend.

Settle the stopping distance before the run. The reach is set by the laser and the night. A 200 mW beam stays usable to roughly 20 to 30 miles on clear, dry air, and falls to 5 to 10 miles when it is hazy or humid.

STEP 04 — THREE READINGS AT END POSITION

The drone reaches the agreed distance and lands. The chase crew takes a fresh Polaris reading, same clinometer, same method as Step 01. The altitude log comes off the drone. The tilt reading comes off the operator's encoder.

Those three end values, set beside their three starting values, are the whole output. Everything after this is comparison.

WHY THREE MEASUREMENTS AND NOT ONE

Any one instrument can lie. A clinometer can be miscalibrated. A rangefinder can carry a software bias. An encoder can slip a tooth. One number, on its own, is hard to trust and easy to argue with.

So log three. Three quantities from three unrelated physical principles: the angle to a star, the distance to the ground, the pitch of a mounted laser. If a real signal is there, all three carry it, and they carry it the same way. If one of them wanders off from the other two, the suspect is that instrument or that step, not the world. The experiment checks itself.

MEASUREMENT 1 — POLARIS ANGLE

Polaris sits within about half a degree of the celestial north pole, and its height above the horizon tracks the observer's latitude (Bowditch, 2019). A change in that angle from start to end is a change in latitude, a step north or south across the surface.

This one comes with a caveat, and it is fair to state it plainly: people who hold different models of the sky read a star's angle differently. So the weight here does not rest on Polaris alone. It rests on whether Polaris agrees with Measurements 2 and 3.

MEASUREMENT 2 — DRONE ALTITUDE ABOVE GROUND

This is the plainest of the three. The drone follows a straight beam of light. If the ground stays the same distance below the beam for the whole flight, the surface is flat across that span. If the ground falls away from the beam, the surface curves across it. The reading does not depend on which way the result comes out. It only reports the distance.

No star to interpret. No claim about how light travels through space. A rangefinder pointed down, logging the height to the ground, second after second, for the length of the flight.

MEASUREMENT 3 — LASER TILT ANGLE

Measurement 3 is Measurement 2 read from the far end of the beam. If the drone climbs, the operator tilts the laser up to keep it lit, and the encoder records the tilt. The total change in tilt is the angle the drone's altitude change subtends back at the mount.

Because it watches the same thing from the other side, it is a cross-check on the second reading. If altitude says the drone rose 600 feet and tilt says it rose 100, one of the two instruments is wrong, and the run waits until you find out which.

READING THE RESULTS

Take the three outcomes at face value, without deciding ahead of time which one you hope to see. Outcome A across all three is a flat reading over the run: the surface held level beneath the beam. Outcome B across all three is a curved reading, the ground falling away in the direction a sphere would produce. Outcome C is the surprising case: Polaris higher than it started, or the ground rising toward the beam rather than falling away. That pattern is not predicted by either a level or a curved surface, so it points back at the setup, not outward at a conclusion.

The result that matters is agreement. When the three readings line up, you have a measurement worth reporting, whatever it says. When they disagree, the run is telling you something was off, and the answer is to fix it and run again, not to pick the number you liked best.

HANDLING ATMOSPHERIC REFRACTION

Atmospheric refraction is the strongest objection to this experiment, and it deserves a direct answer rather than a dismissal. Light bends as it crosses air of changing density, and that bending can imitate the signal the experiment is after, or cover it up. The honest goal is not to wave the problem away. It is to bound it, measure it, and show that what is left over is small.

RUN IN MULTIPLE CONDITIONS

Refraction follows the temperature gradient, the humidity, and the pressure, and those shift from night to night (Hirt et al., 2010). So run on nights that differ. If a cold dry winter run and a warm humid summer run land on the same altitude gain within a small margin, refraction is not the thing driving the number. If the readings swing with the weather, refraction is doing real work, and the signal is not yet trustworthy.

MEASURE REFRACTION DIRECTLY

Refraction can be measured, not just feared. Fire a second laser between two fixed points of known height and distance, in the same air, on the same night. Surveyors do a version of this with reciprocal leveling: two instruments sight each other at the same moment from known positions, and whatever remains after the known geometry is removed is the bending (Hirt et al., 2010). Log that coefficient during the run and take it back out of the altitude reading.

LOG THE AIR AT BOTH ENDS

The temperature, humidity, and pressure sensors on the mount and the drone measure the cause of refraction directly. The bending of the beam is set by how refractivity changes along the path and, most of all, with height (Hirt et al., 2010). Convert each reading to N and you have the air at both ends as a single number. Better still, let the drone hold station and sample at two heights a few metres apart: the change in N over that height is the vertical gradient, and the vertical gradient is what curves the beam. Measured that way, the refraction for the moment is a reading rather than a guess, and it drops straight into the correction beside the two-color and reciprocal checks.

USE MULTIPLE WAVELENGTHS

Refraction depends on color. Red light bends less than blue through the same air (Young, n.d.). Fly a red beam and a green beam to the same drone at once. If refraction is present, the two will not agree, in their tilt or in where they fall, and the size of the disagreement is the size of the refraction. If they sit on top of each other, refraction is negligible. Either way, two colors measure the effect instead of assuming it.

CHOOSE THE NIGHT CAREFULLY

Refraction is worst in the thin layer near the ground, where the temperature gradient is steepest (Young, n.d.). A cold, clear, dry night flattens that gradient. The ridge mount lifts the beam up out of the worst of the layer. And sunrise and sunset are the hours to avoid, when the air near the ground is heating or cooling fastest.

REVERSE THE EXPERIMENT

Run it backward too. Fire from the far point back to the origin, with a second drone going the other way. If both directions show the same altitude pattern, the refraction is symmetric and mostly cancels. If the two directions disagree, you have either an instrument fault or air that is not the same coming and going, and either one is worth chasing down before you draw a line under the result.

THE HONEST LIMIT

You cannot remove refraction without a vacuum, and there is no vacuum out here. What you can do is bound it, measure it, and correct for it until the leftover is small next to the signal. A two-color run, from a high ridge, on a cold clear night, checked in both directions, drives the refraction contribution well under the curvature contribution at moderate range. State the residual that remains. Do not report it as zero.

It also helps to know that refraction has no fixed direction. Its sign and size depend on the vertical temperature profile on the night, not on any model of the surface. In common, well-mixed air it bends light slightly downward, so a distant object sits a little higher than bare geometry alone would place it. Over a strongly heated surface the gradient reverses and it bends light upward, so the object sinks and more of it is lost (an inferior mirage). Over a cold surface under an inversion it can bend much more than usual, lifting into view objects that geometry would hide (looming and ducting) (Young, n.d.). Because it runs both ways, neither a flat reading nor a curved one can be charged to refraction by simply asserting it after the fact. That is the whole reason for the two-color run, the reversal, and the ridge mount: they turn refraction into a number measured on the night and stated as a residual, rather than a correction either side reaches for when the result is inconvenient.
