Methodology ▶

All formulas use effective Earth radius R' = R/(1−k) to model atmospheric refraction. Lines in grey below each formula show the live calculation for your current inputs.

d₁ = √(h² + 2R'h)  [horizon distance]

Distance from eye height h to the horizon. It uses R', so this is the apparent (refracted) horizon; with k=0 it is the geometric horizon.

h₁ = √(d₂² + R'²) − R'  [hidden height]

Height obscured by curvature. d₂ = d₀ − d₁ is the distance beyond your horizon.

B = R'(1 − cos(d/2R'))  [bulge]

Maximum surface rise between observer and target.

dip = acos(R'/(R'+h)) ≈ √(2h/R')  [horizon dip]

The angle (reported here in arcminutes) by which the sea horizon sits below true level. The horizon does not "rise to eye level" as you climb; it sinks farther below it, which is exactly what this dip measures.

Refraction (k): the coefficient of terrestrial refraction, the ratio of Earth's curvature to the light ray's curvature (k = R/r, the optical convention; not to be confused with the radio "k-factor" R'/R = 4/3 used for the same standard air). Air is denser low down, so rays bend gently downward, and the model folds that into geometry by replacing R with an effective radius R' = R/(1−k). Physically k is set by the near-surface temperature gradient: k ≈ 503·(P/T²)·(0.0343 + dT/dh), with P in hPa, T in K, and dT/dh in K/m. The constant 0.0343 K/m is the autoconvective lapse rate, at which air density is uniform and light is not bent (k=0). Ordinary near-surface air runs about k = 0.13 (the long-standing surveying value) to 0.17 (typical visual conditions); steep surface inversions drive it much higher.

Apparent vs geometric horizon: The geometric horizon is the tangent on a smooth airless Earth, at √(2Rh). Refraction bends the line of sight down to graze the surface farther away, so the horizon you actually see, the apparent (visible) horizon, sits at √(2R'h), beyond and slightly above the geometric one. The stronger the refraction, the farther the apparent horizon recedes; under a strong inversion it can lie beyond distant objects, so a low shoreline view shows ships or rigs standing in front of the horizon. Both views label these separately.

Extreme refraction: Near the surface (especially over water) k is highly variable, measured from negative values up to far beyond 0.5 during temperature inversions. These produce looming / superior mirages that can briefly reveal objects normally hidden. This is a separate, intermittent effect, not steady refraction. The k=0.5 value corresponds to a surface inversion of about +0.05 °C/m, which makes the ray bend roughly half as much as the Earth curves; about +0.11 °C/m makes the ray match Earth's curvature exactly (k=1, the onset of ducting) (Young, SDSU).

Laser line-of-sight over water: The Laser Test view uses the same effective-radius geometry. A straight ray clears the surface when its minimum height above the water stays positive; the bulge of the chord above the curved surface at the midpoint is d²/8R'. A horizontal ray rises above the surface as (R'+h)/cos(x/R') − R' ≈ h + x²/2R'. Treating the ray as straight on radius R' = R/(1−k) is exact when the refractivity gradient is constant (ITU-R P.834).

Why real laser tests over water are unreliable: a grazing beam travels through the lowest few meters of air, where the temperature gradient and therefore k are largest and most variable. As k rises toward 1 (a surface inversion of roughly +0.11 °C/m) the ray bends as much as the Earth curves, so it stays level and can travel beyond the horizon as a duct (Young, SDSU). The same sight line then gives different results morning vs afternoon, calm vs windy, which is the signature of changing refraction rather than a fixed geometry. Surveyors handle the steady part with a fixed combined curvature-and-refraction correction of about 0.0675·d² m (d in km), with k ≈ 0.13 measured for over a century (Hirt et al. 2010). The dependable curvature test raises the sight line and uses equal-height targets, as Wallace did on the Bedford Level in 1870 and the 2018 Rainy Lake experiment repeated (Rainy Lake); that is what the Side and Observer views model. A companion drone-and-laser field protocol sketches how to run such a measurement yourself.

Spherical vs parabolic: This uses exact spherical formulas. The "8 inches per mile²" rule is a parabolic approximation of drop below the tangent line; it ignores observer height and overstates what is hidden. The correct hidden-height formula above accounts for both.

Common errors this tool avoids:

  • The obstruction is the horizon, not the bulge: drop below the tangent line (d²/2R') is not the hidden height. Hidden height is measured from the horizon line of sight.
  • "8 inches per mile²" is that tangent drop, and it ignores eye height entirely, so it is not what an observer of any real height sees.
  • The effective radius R' = R/(1−k) is applied to every term (horizon, hidden, bulge, dip), not mixed with the geometric R.
  • Horizon distance uses the exact √(h(h+2R')), not the small-angle √(2R'h), so it stays correct at aircraft and balloon heights.
  • The grazing case (target nearer than the horizon) is clamped to zero hidden, not a small negative number.
  • The "7/6 Earth radius" is exactly k=0.143; k=0.13 is the Gauss mean. The radio "4/3 radius" (k=0.25) is a microwave value, not optical, and is offered as a separate mode.

Observer view optics: The first-person view is rendered as a camera. Focal length f and sensor size set the field of view, FOV = 2·atan(sensor / 2f), and the magnification, f / 50 mm versus a naked eye. Scrolling to zoom changes f like a real lens: it magnifies the scene but cannot lift anything back above the horizon that curvature has already hidden. That matters, since "just zoom in" is a common objection.

Earth model: Default radius is the IUGG mean R = 6,371 km. WGS84 mode uses the latitude-dependent geocentric radius of the reference ellipsoid (a = 6,378.137 km equator → b = 6,356.752 km poles). A Flat plane option (R = ∞) is included as a comparison baseline: with no curvature there is no horizon dip, no bulge, and nothing is ever hidden, so every target stays fully visible at any distance. Switching between it and the sphere shows the difference directly, and that difference is the curvature.

Assumptions & limits:

  • Smooth sphere/ellipsoid at one datum, with no terrain, waves, tides, or local elevation between observer and target.
  • A single uniform k along the whole path (the real atmosphere is layered; the effective-radius method is the standard approximation, best for modest distances and small k).
  • Heights are measured above that datum; the light ray is modeled as a circular arc of radius r = R/k (≈ 7–8× Earth's radius at standard k), which the effective-radius substitution turns into a straight line on radius R'.
  • Ignores diffraction, and treats refraction as a single constant. Real air bends light by a profile that varies with height and weather.

Visual range (haze): Optional. Atmospheric extinction limits how far you can see at all, independent of curvature. Using the Koschmieder relation, contrast falls as exp(−3.912·d/V), where V is the meteorological visual range. Even when geometry leaves a target above the horizon, haze may erase it. Set V=0 to ignore.

Terrain and sea state: Optional. A sight line that just grazes a nearer high point is obstructed by it rather than by the Earth horizon. The tool checks two extra grazing points: an intervening obstacle (a hill or ridge at a set distance and height) and the wave crests that form the visible sea edge. For each, the hidden height on the target is h0 + slope·d0 + drop(d0), where the slope runs from the eye to the grazing point and drop(x) = R'(1 − cos(x / R')) is the surface fall at distance x. Whichever of the Earth horizon, the wave crests, or the obstacle hides the most sets the result, and the panel reports which one. The wave model is a first approximation: it raises the horizon grazing point by the wave height. Set the inputs to 0 to ignore.

Ray-traced refraction: Optional. The effective-radius method treats bending as one fixed number for the whole path. Real air does not: the refractivity gradient changes with height, and near the surface it can reverse. The ray-trace option integrates the light path through a layered profile, solving d²h/ds² = 1/R + dn/dh(h), where h is height above the surface, s is ground distance, and dn/dh is set by the refractivity gradient (in N-units; n = 1 + N·10⁻⁶). A constant gradient of −R⁻¹·k reproduces the effective-radius model exactly, which is why the standard profile overlays the constant-k sight line. A gradient steeper than about −157 N/km bends the ray as fast as the Earth curves; beyond that, light is trapped in a duct, the horizon extends, and distant objects loom or invert. A hot surface reverses the gradient near the ground and bends rays upward, shortening the horizon and producing an inferior mirage. The inversion and mirage profiles apply such a layer near the surface and integrate the result, then report the ray-traced hidden height beside the constant-k value. This is a 2D profile model, not a full 3D atmosphere, and the layer values are representative rather than measured.

Photo overlay (experimental): The Observer View can load a real photograph as a background. Because the view maps the horizontal field of view (2·arctan(sensor / 2·focal)) across the full frame, a photo shot at a known focal length and sensor size shares the same angular scale as the overlays. When a JPEG carries EXIF, the focal length and sensor width are read automatically (the sensor is derived from the 35mm-equivalent focal length, or from the focal-plane resolution tags); otherwise you enter them. After you register the photo's horizon against the orange horizon line, the predicted hidden/visible split lands on the real scene at the correct pixel height. The alignment nudges (move, roll, scale) only register the image; a roll control levels a tilted horizon, and fine scale absorbs lens distortion and EXIF rounding. None of these feed back into any calculation, so treat it as a comparison aid rather than a measurement.

What this does not show: it predicts the geometry of what curvature hides. It cannot tell you whether a specific photo is genuine clear-air, a mirage, or intervening terrain; those depend on conditions on the day.

Glossary (terms link to neutral, scientific definitions)

Horizon
The circle all around you where your line of sight grazes the surface tangentially. It is not a single point, an edge, or a wall: in every direction the surface simply curves out of view at this distance and keeps going beyond it, so tall objects past it (mountains, ships' masts) still rise above it. As you climb, this circle widens, its distance growing with the square root of eye height.
Hidden height
How much of a distant object is blocked by the bulge of the Earth: the vertical amount, measured up from its base, that lies below the observer's line of sight to the horizon. Everything above it stays visible.
Astronomical (true) horizon
The horizontal plane through your eye ("eye level"), perpendicular to straight up. The sea horizon is not at eye level; it sits below it by the dip.
Geometric vs apparent horizon
The geometric horizon is where the surface curves out of view on an airless Earth (tangent at √(2Rh)). The apparent (visible) horizon is what you actually see, pushed farther out and slightly up by refraction (√(2R'h)).
Bulge
The rise of the surface midway between two points: the "hump" a line of sight must clear.
Horizon dip
The small angle (measured in arcminutes, not a distance) by which the visible horizon sits below true eye level for a raised observer. Look out perfectly level and the sea horizon is actually a touch below your gaze; this is by how much.
Atmospheric refraction
Bending of light as it crosses air whose density changes with height. Near the ground the denser air is below, so rays curve gently downward, lifting distant objects slightly, shrinking the horizon dip, and pushing the visible horizon farther out.
Refraction coefficient (k)
Ratio of Earth's curvature to the light ray's curvature; ~0.13 standard, but highly variable near the surface.
Effective Earth radius (R')
An inflated radius R/(1−k) that folds steady refraction into the geometry.
Looming / superior mirage
Strong refraction in a temperature inversion that lifts hidden objects into view; intermittent, not steady.
Ducting
The extreme of refraction (k ≥ 1, an inversion steeper than about +0.11 °C/m): the ray curves as much as the Earth, or more, so light is trapped near the surface and can be seen far beyond the geometric horizon.
Meteorological visibility / visual range
Distance at which contrast fades to the detection threshold (Koschmieder relation). Haze limit.
Reference ellipsoid (WGS84)
The oblate-spheroid model of Earth used for precise, latitude-dependent radii.
Earth radius
Earth is not a perfect sphere; its radius ranges ~6,357 km (poles) to ~6,378 km (equator), mean ~6,371 km.
Line of sight / slant range
The straight path from observer to target, slightly longer than the distance measured along the ground.
